The Arsenal

Gibson Les Paul Studio

The heavyweight champion of rock. This mahogany-bodied beast with its carved maple cap delivers the thick, creamy sustain that powered everything from Page's mystical riffs to Slash's scorching solos. The dual humbucker configuration provides that classic "woman tone" - warm, vocal, and endlessly expressive.

When plugged into a cranked Marshall, the Les Paul transforms into a sonic weapon capable of both delicate jazz phrasings and earth-shaking power chords. It's the guitar that taught me that sometimes, more mass equals more class.

MAHOGANY BODY 490R/498T PICKUPS ENDLESS SUSTAIN

Fender Stratocaster

The Swiss Army knife of guitars. Three single-coil pickups, five-way switching, and that legendary contoured body make this the most versatile instrument in the arsenal. From Hendrix's "Star Spangled Banner" to Gilmour's ethereal soundscapes, the Strat has been the voice of innovation.

The glassy neck pickup, the quacky in-between positions, and that bridge pickup bite - each position is a different flavor of tonal nirvana. Add a whammy bar, and you've got a time machine that can transport you from 1954 to the future and back.

ALDER BODY VINTAGE SINGLE-COILS TREMOLO SYSTEM

Fender Telecaster

Bruce Springsteen called it "the bastard stepchild of electric guitars," but this workhorse has more character than any guitar has a right to. No frills, no nonsense - just pure, cutting tone that slices through any mix like a hot knife through butter.

From Keef's open-G raunch to Roy Buchanan's crying leads, the Tele proves that simplicity is the ultimate sophistication. That bridge pickup twang has launched a thousand country songs, while the neck position delivers jazz tones that would make Wes Montgomery smile.

ASH BODY BROADCASTER PICKUPS FIXED BRIDGE

ES-335 Semi-Hollow

The best of both worlds - the woody resonance of an acoustic meets the power and sustain of a solid body. This is the guitar that BB King rode to glory, that Chuck Berry duck-walked across stages with, and that continues to define the sound of blues and jazz-rock fusion.

The semi-hollow construction adds harmonics and overtones that solid bodies can only dream of. Plug it into a warm tube amp, roll off the tone knob slightly, and you're in a sonic paradise where every note blooms like a flower in spring.

Guitar Gods

Jimi Hendrix

The Revolutionary

The southpaw who played a right-handed Strat upside down and inside out, literally and figuratively. Jimi didn't just play the guitar; he channeled electricity through it, turning feedback into art and noise into poetry. His four-year reign changed music forever.

Eric Clapton

Slowhand

From the Yardbirds to Cream to his solo career, Clapton has been the blueprint for blues-rock guitar. His "woman tone" and emotional vibrato taught us that it's not about how many notes you play, but how you make each one count. Pure soul through six strings.

Steve Vai

The Alien

Frank Zappa's "little Italian virtuoso" who transcended technical mastery to create music from another dimension. With his Ibanez JEM and a wall of effects, Vai paints sonic landscapes that shouldn't be possible with just six strings and ten fingers.

Joe Satriani

The Professor

The teacher who became the master. Satch taught Kirk Hammett and Steve Vai, then went on to show the world that instrumental rock could be both technically brilliant and emotionally moving. His chrome dome and silver surfer guitar are icons of modern rock.

Guthrie Govan

The Polymath

The British wizard who speaks every musical language fluently. From bebop to djent, classical to country, Govan plays it all with a casual brilliance that makes other guitarists question their life choices. His fretboard knowledge is encyclopedic, his execution flawless.

David Gilmour

The Architect

The man who proved that space between notes is as important as the notes themselves. Gilmour's soaring bends and ethereal tones with Pink Floyd created sonic cathedrals. His black Strat has sung some of the most memorable solos in rock history.
